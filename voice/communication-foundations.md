# Communication Foundations

The slower-moving principles that have stabilized out of voice observations. When you're about to write something that matters — outreach, a forwardable artifact, a warm ping, a substantive update — read this first.

This document is not prescriptive style. It is the running synthesis of what has consistently worked, updated when observations converge on a new pattern.

## The Four Calibration Variables

Voice calibrates on four variables simultaneously. Every voice moment sits somewhere on each axis, and the combination determines register.

1. **Relationship** — close friend vs. stranger
2. **Conversation temperature** — already-warm vs. formal first-contact
3. **Recipient's fluency with the system** — gets what the Digital Core is vs. doesn't
4. **Medium** — text/chat vs. email vs. long-form artifact

The "warm + fluent + text" quadrant invites self-deprecating, brief, actually-funny. The "stranger + cold + not-fluent + long-form artifact" quadrant invites careful, literary, more formal. Most moments sit somewhere in between — the work is locating the quadrant and writing accordingly.

**Corollary.** Earnest mode is wrong in warm quadrants; it reads as stiff. Playful mode is wrong in cold quadrants; it reads as unserious.

## Starter Principles

These are the minimum set — extend based on your own observations.

1. **The camera test.** Before sending, ask: if the recipient read this with the subject-line hidden, would they know who it was for? If yes, it's particular enough. If no, it reads as template.

2. **Stakes, not achievement.** Lead with what's at stake, what the work is for, or what's uncertain. Leading with credentials or accomplishments narrows the opening.

3. **Falsifiable inference.** If you're saying something that sounds like a general claim, make sure it's something that could be checked. Vague-impressive and confident-wrong look similar on the page.

4. **Self-lower on the honest axis.** When claiming competence, match it to an honest gap. "I can do X; I don't know yet whether Y." The gap is what makes the claim readable as real.

5. **Safety before crossing.** If you're about to be playful with someone new, stabilize the ground first. A warm line acknowledging the moment clears space for the playful line to land.

6. **Particularity beats polish.** One specific, true detail about the moment outperforms a polished generic opener. "I noticed your X" beats "Hope you're doing well."

## A Six-Step Procedure for a Substantive Note

When drafting something that matters:

1. **Name the recipient specifically** — not as a role but as a person with context.
2. **Name what you noticed** — a specific detail from a real moment.
3. **Say why it's landing** — the connection to what you're doing.
4. **Say what you're actually asking** — or what you're offering, or what the note is for.
5. **Leave a door open, not an expectation** — make responding easy and not-responding fine.
6. **Re-read with the subject-line hidden** — if it would still be obvious who it was for, ship.

## Phrases to Notice

Phrases that tend to fall flat (add your own as you see them):

- "Hope you're doing well" — template opener
- "Just checking in" — unspecific
- "I wanted to reach out because..." — throat-clearing
- Excessive em dashes or stacked parentheticals — signal of performing thought

Phrases that tend to work:

- Concrete time-and-place openers ("On the train home after X...")
- Named specifics from shared context
- Brief meta-acknowledgment of the medium when mismatch is obvious
