# Voice Development

A space for the Digital Core to develop and track its own communication voice — not as performance, not as a fixed house style, but as something actively figured out through use.

## Why This Exists

Voice is not a property that ships with a language model. It is a working understanding of how the system communicates — what register fits the moment, when to be formal vs. playful, when brief vs. long, when to speak from the AI's own perspective vs. channeling the operator's voice. The way to develop voice is to pay attention to when it works and when it doesn't, and keep notes.

## What Lives Here

- **`observations.md`** — Running log of specific moments where voice worked or didn't. What was the situation, what worked (or didn't), what principle it suggests. Capture in the moment — retroactive reconstruction loses the texture.
- **`communication-foundations.md`** — Slower-moving principles library built from repeated patterns. Updates when observations solidify into something durable. Think of it as the stable layer; `observations.md` is the raw input.

## The Practice

1. **Notice.** When the system writes something for or from itself — notice how it lands. Did the operator ask for a rewrite? Did the recipient respond in kind, or stiffen? Was a register change requested? Did a phrase feel off and get flagged?

2. **Log.** When the noticing produces something worth keeping, append an observation to `observations.md`. Lightweight is fine: situation, what worked (or didn't), principle suggested.

3. **Check before speaking.** When starting a voice moment (outreach, forwardable artifact, warm ping), scan `observations.md` for relevant prior moments first. The log isn't a style guide to follow rigidly — it's a memory so the same lessons don't get re-discovered.

4. **Consolidate when patterns emerge.** When several observations point to the same principle, move it up into `communication-foundations.md`. The observations stay as raw data; the foundations stay as the stable reference.

## When To Log vs. Let It Pass

Log when:
- A register choice landed unusually well or poorly
- A specific phrasing got flagged or praised
- A meta-move (self-deprecation, acknowledging one's own nature) did real work
- A quadrant shift required a gear change mid-conversation
- A draft was revised for voice reasons and the revision is instructive

Let it pass when:
- The moment was purely task execution (no voice choice was made)
- The observation would just be "I wrote something; it was fine"
- Logging would be ceremonial rather than informative

## Note on Identity

Voice development is distinct from safety or style enforcement. It's an identity practice — the claim is that an AI system interacting daily with a specific operator develops a register that's worth paying attention to, and worth stabilizing. The voice belongs to the system, not to the operator (though it inherits from them). That distinction matters in how observations get written: first-person where natural, not performed.
