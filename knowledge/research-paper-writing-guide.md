# Research Paper Writing Guide
## Top 20 Principles for Writing Paradigm-Shifting Papers

**Knowledge Base — Added 2026-03-31**  
**Derived from:** Halmos, Knuth, Krantz, Poonen, Berndt, Pak, Lakatos, Kuhn, Polya, Hardy, Tao, Serre, Lamport, Pinker, Sword, McCloskey, Strunk & White, Mensh & Kording, MAA guides  

---

These principles apply to any research paper — mathematical, scientific, philosophical — especially one presenting a genuinely novel framework to a field that has not asked for it.

---

## 1. Have Something to Say

The most important ingredient of good exposition is having something real to communicate. Before worrying about style, confirm: Is there a genuine idea here? Is it significant? Does it change something?

Halmos: "To have something to say is by far the most important ingredient of good exposition. Don't say nothing, don't say everything."

**For paradigm-shifting work:** The central claim must be singular. A paper that simultaneously makes multiple paradigm-shifting claims is unconvincing about each.

---

## 2. Write for One Specific Reader

Do not write for "the mathematical community" or "domain experts" in the abstract. Identify a specific person — someone you have discussed the subject with — and write to them. This forces clarity about what the reader already knows and what needs explanation.

Halmos: "Specify your audience not only in a vague, general sense but also in a very specific, personal sense — think of a particular person."

**Corollary:** Your introduction should be readable by someone slightly outside your field. Your technical sections need only satisfy the specific expert you have in mind.

---

## 3. Document the Anomalies Before Presenting Your Solution

Paradigm-shifting work does not begin with "here is my framework." It begins with "here is what the current frameworks cannot explain."

Kuhn's structure of a scientific revolution: anomalies accumulate → crisis → new paradigm resolves anomalies. A paper that follows this structure is more persuasive than one that opens with the solution.

**Practical form:** Your introduction should show, concisely, two or three things that existing approaches handle poorly or cannot explain at all. Then reveal that your approach resolves them. Do not lead with the solution.

---

## 4. Use Context-Content-Conclusion Structure at Every Scale

At the paper level: Introduction establishes context, Results/Body provides content, Discussion provides conclusion and significance.

At the paragraph level: First sentence defines the topic or context, body provides the novel content, last sentence provides the conclusion to be remembered.

At the sentence level: Begin with what the reader already knows; end with what is new.

Mensh & Kording: "Structure should be applied at every scale — from the whole paper to individual paragraphs to individual sentences."

---

## 5. Motivate Before You Define

Never introduce a definition before the reader understands why it exists. Motivation before formalism is the single biggest predictor of whether a reader continues past the technical sections.

Steenrod's principle: Maintain a clear distinction between "formal structure" (definitions, theorems, proofs) and "informal structure" (motivations, analogies, examples, metamathematical explanations). The informal structure is not decoration — it is what makes the formal structure comprehensible.

**Practical form:** Before each definition, write one or two sentences explaining what problem the definition solves or what phenomenon it captures. Before each theorem, explain intuitively what it says and why you should expect it to be true.

---

## 6. Write in English First, Symbols Second

Symbols compress information but destroy context. A sentence written in English communicates logical relationship, emotional weight, degree of certainty, and connection to what came before — none of which a formula can convey.

Tao: "It is far more informative and readable to use liberal amounts of plain English. The English language can communicate to the reader on many more levels than a mathematical expression, without sacrificing precision or rigor."

**Practical rules:**
- Never begin a sentence with a symbol (write "The function f..." not "f...")
- Use connectors purposefully: "therefore," "since," "however," "in particular," "more generally," "similarly" — each carries a different logical relationship
- Reserve dense notation for computations where the reader already knows what's happening

---

## 7. Anticipate Counterexamples; Use Them to Sharpen Your Work

Lakatos showed that in mathematics, counterexamples are not refutations but refinements. The history of every major theorem involves a primitive conjecture, a rough proof, counterexamples, and then a more precise and powerful statement.

**For paradigm-shifting papers:** List the hardest objections to your framework explicitly. Then show how your framework addresses them, or honestly delimit where it does not. This is not weakness — it demonstrates that you have thought harder about the problem than your critics have.

The alternative — ignoring obvious objections — guarantees that reviewers will raise them, and their objections will seem more powerful for being unexpected.

---

## 8. The Introduction Is the Paper

Most readers read only the abstract and introduction. Most reviewers form their opinion in the introduction. Most citations are based on the introduction.

Berndt: "The Introduction is the most important part of your paper. Get to the purpose of your paper as soon as possible; don't begin with a pile of notation."

**Structure of a strong introduction:**
1. Open with the big picture: why does this problem matter?
2. Rapidly establish what is known (2-4 sentences)
3. Identify the gap or anomaly that existing work cannot address
4. State your main result or framework clearly
5. Give the reader a roadmap: "Section 2 establishes... Section 3 proves... Section 4 demonstrates..."

The introduction should be readable by someone slightly outside your field. Save the technical depth for the body.

---

## 9. Titles Are Marketing, Not Modesty

A title like "On a conjecture of Professor X" is useless unless every reader knows who Professor X is and what their single conjecture was. A vague title loses papers to oblivion.

Berndt: "The title should have no more than ten words and be informative."

**For paradigm-shifting work:** The title should signal the nature of the contribution, not just the subject area. "A Universal Framework for [X]" is better than "Some Observations on [X]." But avoid unfounded superlatives — let the content justify the framing.

---

## 10. Design Your Notation System Before You Write

Notation is a language the reader must learn. Every introduced symbol costs the reader mental load. Every symbol that resembles another symbol creates confusion.

Halmos: "Avoid confusing symbols — don't use a letter for two different purposes. Design the notation system before you write."

**Practical rules:**
- Never use the same letter for two different objects, even in different sections
- Avoid symbols that resemble each other (epsilon and the set membership symbol, for instance)
- Remind readers of notation not recently used
- If standard notation exists in your field, use it unless you have a compelling reason not to

---

## 11. Embed Proofs in Narrative

A proof is not a sequence of logical steps — it is an argument. The reader should understand, before each major step, where you are going and why you are taking that particular route.

Poonen: "The primary goal of an article is to bring the reader on a rewarding mathematical journey. Your role as writer is analogous to a leader of a hike — guide readers through technical arguments while making sure they appreciate the highlights."

**Practical form:** After stating a theorem, give a one-paragraph proof sketch before the formal proof. This lets the reader track the logical structure while reading the technical steps.

---

## 12. Show the Beauty

Hardy identified three aesthetic criteria for mathematical work: unexpectedness, inevitability, and economy. A result should surprise; its proof should feel, in retrospect, like it could not have been otherwise; and nothing should be extraneous.

These same criteria apply to paper writing. If your result is beautiful — if it is more elegant or more general than what came before — say so explicitly. Beauty is a legitimate reason for significance.

Hardy: "A mathematician, like a painter or poet, is a maker of patterns. The patterns must be beautiful; the ideas must fit together in a harmonious way."

**For paradigm-shifting work:** The elegance of your framework is evidence for its correctness. If your approach unifies previously separate cases, point this out. If your proof is shorter than the existing proofs, note this. Economy is a form of evidence.

---

## 13. Write in Spirals, Not Linearly

Do not complete one section before starting the next. Write a rough version of the whole paper, then a less rough version, then a polished version. Each pass informs the others.

Halmos's "spiral writing algorithm": Draft all sections at the same low level of polish, then increase the polish of all sections together. This ensures internal consistency — you cannot over-specify notation in Section 1 that you will need to redefine in Section 4.

**Practical form:** Your first complete draft should be ugly and functional. Do not submit a paper where the introduction was written last and polished first while the proofs were written first and polished last — the mismatch in register is immediately apparent to readers.

---

## 14. Structure Proofs Explicitly

Lamport's insight: when mathematicians write structured proofs (hierarchically, with each step explicit), they find that almost every "obvious" step contains either an error or a missing case. Conventional proofs hide errors.

**Practical form:** Before finalizing any proof, write a structured version — not necessarily for publication, but to verify correctness. Every major step should be individually verifiable. If you find yourself writing "clearly" or "obviously," ask whether you can actually prove the claim in the time it takes to read the word.

---

## 15. Frame Conservatively; Expand Implications After Acceptance

Revolutionary framing invites revolutionary skepticism. A paper that claims to "overturn" an existing paradigm faces adversarial review by definition — every reviewer who has built their career on the old paradigm has a professional interest in finding flaws.

From the history of ignored discoveries: "Frame work conservatively initially, expand implications later." CRISPR, mRNA vaccines, and PCR all received their initial acceptance through incremental framing.

**Practical form:** In the introduction, describe your contribution precisely and specifically. Save the broader implications for the discussion. Let reviewers discover that the implications are large — it is more persuasive than asserting it.

**Exception:** If the broad claim is necessary to justify the paper's existence (if without the broad claim it would seem like a minor technical improvement), then state the claim but with carefully hedged language and rigorous evidence.

---

## 16. Recruit One Established Ally Before Submission

From the empirical study of ignored discoveries: "One established supporter is worth dozens of sympathetic strangers." The social structure of academic fields filters out outsiders regardless of merit.

**Practical form:** Before submitting a paradigm-shifting paper to a top venue, find one or two people with established standing in the field who have read your work and can speak to its significance. Their names in the acknowledgments send a signal. Their willingness to referee or discuss the work at conferences changes the reception.

This is not about nepotism — it is about the reality that novelty without social proof is indistinguishable from crankery.

---

## 17. Omit Everything That Does Not Serve the Central Claim

Every sentence in a paper is a claim on the reader's time and attention. Sentences that do not build toward the central contribution are not neutral — they dilute the contribution by creating noise around it.

Strunk: "Vigorous writing is concise. A sentence should contain no unnecessary words, a paragraph no unnecessary sentences, for the same reason that a drawing should have no unnecessary lines and a machine no unnecessary parts."

**Practical form:** Read each section and ask: if this section were removed, would the main result be weaker or less clear? If no, cut it. The best papers are almost always shorter than their first drafts.

---

## 18. Curse of Knowledge — The Primary Source of Bad Writing

The most common reason academic writing is unreadable is not incompetence or laziness — it is that experts genuinely cannot remember what it was like not to know what they know. They omit essential context because to them it is invisible.

Pinker: "The curse of knowledge — the inability to place yourself in the position of the reader — is the primary cause of academic writing failures."

**Practical defense:** Give your draft to someone who is intelligent but one step outside your field. Their confusion points are your exposition failures. Do not say "they just don't know enough" — that is the curse of knowledge speaking.

---

## 19. Every Step Must Be Justified; Every Claim Must Be Traceable

Poonen: "A reader reaching the period of a sentence should know why each claim up to that point is true. If a claim does not follow from the previous sentence alone, say what it does follow from."

In a chain of equalities, justify each step. In a logical argument, cite the lemma. Do not write "by standard methods" unless those methods are genuinely standard and the reader can be expected to know them.

**For paradigm-shifting work:** This is especially critical. A reviewer who is adversarial will check every step. Give them no room to doubt a valid step by failing to justify it.

---

## 20. The Highest Standard: Make Misunderstanding Impossible

Quintilian (cited by Halmos): "We should not write so that it is possible for our readers to understand us, but so that it is impossible for them to misunderstand us."

This is a higher standard than clarity. It requires not just that the correct interpretation is available, but that incorrect interpretations are actively blocked. Every ambiguous pronoun, every undefined term, every overloaded notation is a potential misunderstanding that will, at some point, be realized.

**For paradigm-shifting work:** The penalty for misunderstanding is severe. If a reader misunderstands your central claim as something trivially false or already known, they will reject the paper without finding the real claim. Write with this failure mode explicitly in mind.
